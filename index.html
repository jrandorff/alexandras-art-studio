🩷 Axolotl picks

✏️ Realistic

  • Draw what you see, not what you know — look at a photo or real thing while you draw.
  • Shading makes things look round: decide where the light comes from, and darken the other side.
  • Sketch light guide lines first, then darken the lines you like best.
  • It's slow — realistic artists take their time and that's normal.

🩷 Try it: find a photo of a real axolotl and draw its actual spots and gill shapes.

😄 Cartoon

  • Big simple shapes: circles, beans, and eggs make the best cartoon bodies.
  • Exaggerate! Huge eyes, tiny feet, giant smiles — cartoons stretch the truth on purpose.
  • Thick, confident outlines make a drawing feel "finished."
  • Flat colors (no shading) are totally allowed here.

🩷 Try it: draw an axolotl as one big bean shape with a huge happy smile.

👁️ Manga / Anime

  • Eyes are the star: large, shiny, with white sparkle squares in them.
  • Small nose and mouth — sometimes just tiny lines.
  • Hair (or gills!) drawn in pointy sections, like flower petals.
  • Emotions get symbols: sweat drops, sparkles, anger marks.

🩷 Try it: give an axolotl giant sparkly anime eyes and blush lines.

🌊 Watercolor

  • Water first, paint second: wet the paper a little and let colors bloom.
  • Light colors first, dark colors last — you can't easily undo dark.
  • Let blobs and drips happen. "Mistakes" become clouds, water, and magic.
  • Let each layer dry before adding details on top (hard, but worth it!).

🩷 Try it: paint a soft pink axolotl blob, let it dry, then add face details with a thin marker.

🟪 Pixel Art

  • Draw on graph paper: every square is one "pixel" — color squares, never half-squares.
  • Start tiny: 16×16 squares is enough for a whole character.
  • Straight edges and stair-steps are the style, not a mistake.
  • Pick just 3–5 colors before you start.

🩷 Try it: make a 16×16 pixel axolotl — pink squares, two black squares for eyes.
